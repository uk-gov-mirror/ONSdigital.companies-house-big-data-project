Group Strategic Report
for the Year Ended 30 June 2019

The directors present their strategic report of the company and the group for the year ended 30 June 2019.

The principal activity of the company is that of a holding company and the principal activities of the group are
the development and operation of entertainment, leisure and property facilities.

Strategic Management

The last financial year was a year of consolidation for the Group. A significant amount of effort went into
achieving the listing of Bruce Pubs PLC on the Nex Exchange. The aim was to raise debt finance through a
bond raise which would be used to fund an overall expansion of the Group's estate.

Although the bonds were successfully listed and a small amount of funding was raised, ultimately there was
not sufficient appetite from lenders at the scale or at rates which would have made the funding attractive for
expansion.

Instead, the directors have returned their focus to the Group's core trading in the heartland of Edinburgh. The
Group's bankers continue to be supportive and a potential significant purchase is currently under negotiation
which has the backing of the bank.

Although there were no further acquisitions made during the year, the directors have been working on a
number of exciting development projects. One of these is expected to be trading in mid 2020 and will add to
the Group's portfolio within the Edinburgh Old Town.

The last 2 major redevelopment projects which the Group has undertaken have helped demonstrate the
credentials of the management team in this area. Stramash (redeveloped 2015/16) and George IV
(redeveloped 2017/18) have been growing year on year and now contribute significantly to group turnover.

Business Environment

The business is still predominantly focused on the Old Town area of Edinburgh serving both locals and
tourists and this will remain a key trading area. Expansion into other geographic areas will be considered
where acquisitions can be identified that will provide a suitable return and add value to the company.

REVIEW OF BUSINESS
The group grew turnover to 11,594,615 from 7,578,451. The current financial year also benefited from a full
year trading of the refurbished George IV. This has proved to be an extremely popular local venue and sales
were up 1,090,506 on the prior year. The unit was closed for refurbishment for part of the prior year.

The directors undertook a re-appraisal of property values during the year. Although formal 3rd party
valuations were not completed, the directors used an earnings multiple derived from the March 2018
valuations completed by Christie & Co and applied it to the updated 2019 earnings as a starting point. Four
units were revalued, with three increasing and one decreasing in value.

The company generated cash from operations (before tax and interest) of 1,357,120 which was up on the
prior year of 1,008,817. Loans reduced from 6,420,066 to 6,019,224.

EBITDA for the year ending 30th June 2019 was 1,016,355 (2018 : 1,007,856). EBITDA is excluding the
impact of investment property revaluation.

PRINCIPAL RISKS AND UNCERTAINTIES
Although Brexit has now been achieved, there still remains continued uncertainty over how the
implementation of it will affect the UK economy. A significant downturn in UK consumer confidence or a
negative impact on tourist numbers would both adversely impact the Group. Edinburgh remains a significant
tourist destination and the Group benefits from the incremental spending which tourists bring.

The directors have focused on building a managed estate which is built around venues offering live music.
Due to the increasing importance of food in the overall consumer experience, this also forms a key part of the
offering where appropriate. The new unit which is being developed and is due to start trading in the second
half of 2020 will introduce a new style of unit which is more heavily food lead and is more focused on daytime
and early evening trade.

The principal operational risks around the company are the losing of a premises licence on either a short term
or long term basis. The senior management team work hard to ensure that the company is compliant with
national and local laws and maintains good relationships with neighbours in the areas surrounding its
operations.

A significant investment is being made in the new trading unit and any cost or time overruns on the project
would lead to a longer run in to the break-even point. Although the directors are confident that this will prove
to be a successful site, due to its location in the heart of Edinburgh's Old Town with windows looking onto the
Royal Mile, it is a brand new development rather than the upgrade or conversion of an existing trading unit.
With this type of development, there is always the potential that it will not attract the footfall expected.

There are also risks around the introduction of further legislation which may cause operating costs to rise.
Increasing minimum wage costs are one such example. On a local level increased rates assessments also
provide a challenge.

Coronavirus

As at the date of signing, the Coronavirus outbreak is causing considerable uncertainty for all businesses in
the UK, but particularly those in the leisure sector.The directors have taken immediate measures to reduce
costs and preserve cash. Although they expect income and profits to be significantly down for the remainder
of the financial year ending 30th June 2020, they anticipate that the business will recover once the worst of
the outbreak is over. If measures to combat the outbreak were to continue into the Edinburgh Festival
season, then this would have a detrimental impact to earnings at the start of the next financial year. However,
the directors anticipate that although this would be an unwelcome development the measures being taken
and ongoing support from the Group's bankers would allow it to trade through it.

The directors have taken the following measures in order to preserve cash and safeguard the group:-

- Staff have been furloughed as of 23rd March 2020 and the group will be making application to the
government job retention scheme when this becomes available.
- The group will benefit from the reduction in property rates for the rates year 2020/21. This will start to
provide an immediate benefit as rates payments due in April will no longer have to be made.
- Confirmation has been received from the Group's bankers that a repayment holiday on loans will be made
available for 3 months initially.
- A claim for business interruption loss is being initiated under the Group's insurance policy.
- Extended payment terms have been agreed with some of the major suppliers to the Group. These have
been made possible due to the excellent supplier relations enjoyed by the Group and built up over many
years.

At present, the directors do not anticipate that an application under the Coronavirus Business Interruption
Loan Scheme will be required, but this remains a fall-back option, should the closures remain in place longer
than first anticipated.

2. ACCOUNTING POLICIES

Basis of preparing the financial statements
These financial statements have been prepared in accordance with Financial Reporting Standard 102
"The Financial Reporting Standard applicable in the UK and Republic of Ireland" and the Companies
Act 2006. The financial statements have been prepared under the historical cost convention.

The principal accounting policies adopted in the preparation of the financial statements are set out
below and have remained unchanged from the previous year.

Turnover
Turnover represents net invoiced sale of goods, excluding value added tax. For properties held for
rental purposes, rental income is recognised in the period in which it is due. The group's policy is to
recognise a sale when substantively all risks and rewards in connection with the goods have been
passed to the buyer.

Goodwill
Goodwill is comprised of two elements. Goodwill relating to the acquisition of Bruce Taverns and the
acquisition of Bruce Faith is being amortised straight line over 20 years.The second element is
goodwill on the acquisition of a trading unit within Bruce Taverns. This is being amortised straight line
over 10 years.

Tangible fixed assets
Heritable properties and investment properties are being held under the revaluation model. All other
tangible fixed assets are held under the cost model.

The directors believe that the residual value of heritable and investment properties will match or
exceed their current net book values. Therefore no depreciation has been provided in the current year.

Included within heritable property is a Police Box. This is being depreciated at 4% per annum straight
line. Other heritable property is being depreciated at 2% per annum straight line when a provision is
made.

For other asset classes depreciation is provided at the following annual rates in order to write off each
asset over its estimated useful life.

Lease costs capitalised in relation to the leasing of properties are depreciated on a straight line basis
over the duration of the lease.

Short leasehold - Straight line over the duration of the lease.

Fixtures and fittings - 20% on straight line basis.

Motor vehicles - 25% on straight line basis.

Office equipment - 25% on a straight line basis.

Investment property
Investment property is shown at most recent valuation. Any aggregate surplus or deficit arising from
changes in fair value is recognised in profit or loss.

Where a fair value adjustment has been made, the amount has been transferred from retained
earnings to a fair value reserve in order to distinguish between distributable and non distributable
reserves.

2. ACCOUNTING POLICIES - continued

Stocks
Stocks are valued at the lower of cost and net realisable value, after making due allowance for
obsolete and slow moving items.

Financial instruments
The Company only enters into basic financial instrument transactions that result in the recognition of
financial assets and liabilities like trade and other accounts receivable and payable, loans from banks
and other third parties.

Debt instruments (other than those wholly repayable or receivable within one year), including loans and
other accounts receivable and payable are initially measured at present value of future cash flows and
subsequently amortised at cost using the effective interest method. Where loans have been
consolidated, this has been treated as a new loan agreement and the effective rate of interest has
been recalculated.

Creditors payable within one year, typically trade creditors, are measured initially and subsequently at
the undiscounted amount of the cash or other consideration expected to be paid.

Debtors receivable within one year, typically trade debtors, are measured initially and subsequently at
the undiscounted amount of the cash or other consideration expected to be received.

Taxation
Taxation for the year comprises current and deferred tax. Tax is recognised in the Consolidated
Income Statement, except to the extent that it relates to items recognised in other comprehensive
income or directly in equity.

Current or deferred taxation assets and liabilities are not discounted.

Current tax is recognised at the amount of tax payable using the tax rates and laws that have been
enacted or substantively enacted by the balance sheet date.

Deferred tax
Deferred tax is recognised in respect of all timing differences that have originated but not reversed at
the balance sheet date.

Timing differences arise from the inclusion of income and expenses in tax assessments in periods
different from those in which they are recognised in financial statements. Deferred tax is measured
using tax rates and laws that have been enacted or substantively enacted by the year end and that are
expected to apply to the reversal of the timing difference.

Unrelieved tax losses and other deferred tax assets are recognised only to the extent that it is probable
that they will be recovered against the reversal of deferred tax liabilities or other future taxable profits.

Hire purchase and leasing commitments
Assets obtained under hire purchase contracts or finance leases are capitalised in the balance sheet.
Those held under hire purchase contracts are depreciated over their estimated useful lives. Those held
under finance leases are depreciated over their estimated useful lives or the lease term, whichever is
the shorter.

The interest element of these obligations is charged to profit or loss over the relevant period. The
capital element of the future payments is treated as a liability.

Pension costs and other post-retirement benefits
The group operates a defined contribution pension scheme. Contributions payable to the group's
pension scheme are charged to profit or loss in the period to which they relate.
